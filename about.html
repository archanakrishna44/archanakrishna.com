I start every project by becoming temporarily obsessed with the people I'm designing for.

Their workflows, their workarounds, and especially the sticky notes taped to their monitors. The real work is connecting the dots between what stakeholders think they want and what users innately need. It takes equal parts diplomacy and stubbornness, and digging deep to crack a problem wide open.

That curiosity, the pull to understand both the business side and the people side, started early. I began my career in product management at Macys.com, where I learned the importance of analytical thinking and finding the story behind the numbers. But the numbers never explained the why. I felt there had to be more to the story.

The best design strategies come from figuring out which questions to ask, not from having all the answers.

That question led me to discover human-centered design. I spent my early UX years at startups, which taught me how to be resourceful, wear multiple hats, and build products from nothing. Enterprise taught me how to scale that thinking. At Fannie Mae, I spent seven years leading UX research and service design across complex B2B platforms in the multifamily mortgage space. I partnered with product, engineering, and leadership teams to shape platform strategy and led research that influenced multimillion-dollar investment decisions.

Lately, that same puzzle-solving brain has me exploring how systems thinking and process orchestration can be applied to AI, and finding new ways to optimize how I work along the way.
